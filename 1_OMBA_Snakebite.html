Snakebite

Applying perspective change to the Planet Money "Snakebite" story.

The story in brief
In 2001, snake expert Joe Slowinski died in the Myanmar jungle from a krait bite — antivenom couldn't reach him, because it needs refrigeration and must match the exact snake. Over 100,000 people die of snakebite every year, almost always far from a hospital, and there had been no new treatment in a century. A decade later, ER doctor Matt Lewin, prepping for a similar expedition, packed neostigmine — a drug that reverses paralysis — figuring it could buy a bitten victim time to keep breathing. When a field researcher refused to inject it ("I don't want to stick a needle in someone in the jungle"), Lewin kept explaining the needle procedure. Then, on the flight home, it hit him: what if you skip the needle and just spray it up the nose?

Question 3 · Old perspective → new perspective

List as many assumptions as you can in the old perspective on treating snakebite — and for each, how Lewin saw it differently. Then reveal a model answer to compare.

A model answer (many are possible)
  • Old: you must identify the snake and match it to a specific antivenom. New: a general drug (neostigmine) counters the paralysis, whatever the snake.
  • Old: treatment must be injected by a trained professional. New: a nasal spray almost anyone can give.
  • Old: the goal is to neutralize the venom. New: the goal is to buy time to keep breathing and reach help.
  • Old: a new treatment needs Big Pharma and huge trials. New: an ER doctor, a rock musician, and a university lab can move it forward.

Question 6 · Why it's hard

In your own words: why was it so hard for Lewin — and the whole field — to drop the needle, when in hindsight it looks obvious? Write your answer, then compare.

A model answer
We don't just make assumptions — we commit to them and build our craft on them. Injection was Lewin's training as an ER doctor; antivenom was the field's hundred-year identity. A committed perspective feels like reality rather than a choice, so the easy path is to make it work harder (explain the needle better) instead of questioning it. Changing a perspective means admitting the structure you trusted was optional all along — which is exactly what makes creativity difficult.
