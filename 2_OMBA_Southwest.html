1 The $10 Late-Night Fare Worked example

In 1971 most Americans had never set foot on a plane. Flying was a premium service, priced and sold to business travelers and the wealthy. Southwest had empty planes on late-night runs back for maintenance — so it sold those seats for $10, and in walked a brand-new kind of flyer: people who'd otherwise have taken a Greyhound bus.

What did the whole industry assume? (answer shown)

This is a belief about who flies. Southwest didn't accept that the customer base was fixed.

Which PAGES element can we read as the lever — the one whose change forced the rest to be rethought? (answer shown)

Start by redefining the customer from "a premium flyer" to "anyone who'd take a bus," and you're forced to rebuild the whole offer around them.
S · Selves
Default → Change: Customer = a business traveler who can afford premium fares → anyone who could take a Greyhound bus.
Cascade: change who the customer is (Self), and you have to rework the rest to reach them — a new Goal (fill empty seats, not serve premium flyers), a new Part (a $10 price instead of a premium fare), and a new Event (off-peak late-night flights).

2 The 10-Minute Turn

Across the industry, turning a plane around — unloading, cleaning, reloading — took 25 minutes or more, handled by a dedicated ground crew. Then Southwest was forced to sell one of its four planes to stay afloat. Somehow it kept the exact same flight schedule with only three.

What did the industry take for granted?

Which PAGES element can we read as the lever — the one whose change forced the rest to be rethought?

G · Goals
Default → Change: Turnaround goal = clean and reload in 25+ minutes → do it in 10.
Cascade: a 10-minute goal doesn't fix anything by itself — it's impossible the old way, so it forces you to ask what else has to change to hit it. New Actions (everyone cleans and preps, not just a dedicated crew), and even who's on the team: Southwest hired people willing to pitch in, not veteran pilots who'd think cleaning the plane was beneath them (Selves) — all in the same Event.
3 Open Seating

Other airlines used assigned seats and complex boarding sequences (back-to-front), which often jammed the aisle as people hunted for their exact seat. Southwest dropped assigned seats entirely — passengers sit wherever they like, in the order they arrive.

What did the industry take for granted?

Which PAGES element can we read as the lever — the one whose change forced the rest to be rethought?

P · Parts
Default → Change: Seats = pre-assigned numbers → no assignments; passengers board and grab any open seat.
Cascade: drop the seat assignment (Part) and you're forced to rework boarding into a first-come scramble (Action) — which means inventing new Parts (boarding-order groups like A/B/C) and asking passengers to see themselves differently: not as someone who bought a seat, but as someone who bought the right to board and find one (Self).
4 The Single-Type Fleet

After deregulation in 1978, competitors bought different aircraft for different routes — small planes for short hops, big ones for busy corridors — and pushed everyone through major hubs. Southwest flew only the Boeing 737, on every route, into smaller and cheaper airports.

What did the industry take for granted?

Which PAGES element can we read as the lever — the one whose change forced the rest to be rethought?

P · Parts
Default → Change: Aircraft = different types for different routes, flown through big hubs → one type (the Boeing 737), into smaller, cheaper airports.
Cascade: standardize on one 737 (Part) and you can deliberately simplify the rest — train every pilot on every plane and every mechanic on every system (Actions), and staff with interchangeable crews (Selves).
